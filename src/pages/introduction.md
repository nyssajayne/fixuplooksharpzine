This is a zine about making and mending.  I know how to make and mend clothes, while my friends know how to do lots and lots of other things, like fix electronics and grow vegetables.

I learned to sew when I was a teenager in the early 00’s.  I took a hybrid high school/community college class in clothing production and design where I learned to draft paper patterns and make garments.  I let the skill go for a little while but picked it back up around 5 or so years later when I went back to university to complete my bachelors.

Sewing felt punk to me.  I didn’t want to wear what was in fashion and sewing meant I didn’t need to be told.  However, not long after I started sewing again, the Rana Plaza collapse happened in Bangladesh.  It was one of the deadliest garment factory disasters ever, with a death toll of 1,134 people.  At the time, it felt like everyone was talking about it and maybe meaningful change around the production and consumption of fast fashion would happen.

Over 10 years later and fast fashion continues to grow and mutate.  I am not a perfect consumer, but my resolve to avoid fast fashion has strengthened.  It contributes to climate change through its production, then the garments that are made end up in landfills once they start to fail.  And despite all the technical advancements across many industries in the last decade, clothing production is not automated – human hands still make clothes.  Not all working conditions are exploitative, however if the garment is already inexpensive, one has to question how much someone got paid to make it.

So when a friend Andrew asked me if I could alter the sleeves on his jacket and in exchange, he’d sharpen my kitchen knives, I said yes straight away.  Mending and making do is important, but so is personal style – this jacket was Andrew’s favourite jacket and he didn’t want to part with it.  And now, here we are!
